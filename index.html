Global Spirits: Crisis, Capital & Opportunity

Where capital and opportunity flow as simultaneous domestic contraction, distillery closures, retaliatory tariffs, and geopolitical disruption reshape the global spirits landscape over the next 3–5 years.

Investment Thesis

The Great Spirits Restructuring

The global spirits industry is experiencing its most significant structural realignment since Prohibition. Six simultaneous forces are creating a once-in-a-generation opportunity landscape for investors who can read the map:

▼ U.S. De-Premiumization + ✖ Retaliatory Tariff Walls + 🏭 Accelerating Closures + 🌍 Geopolitical Fractures + 💰 Distressed Asset Prices = → Capital Reallocation Event

Executive Summary

The U.S. spirits industry—the world’s largest by revenue—reported supplier revenues of $37.2 billion in 2024, down 1.1% from $37.7 billion in 2023 and well off the ~$39.1 billion peak of 2022 (DISCUS). Volumes actually rose 1.1% to 312.2 million nine-liter cases, meaning the market is undergoing de-premiumization: consumers drinking roughly the same amount but trading down in price tier. The high-end premium and super-premium segments declined 5.6% in 2024. This revenue-volume divergence is the defining signal of the current cycle.

Simultaneously, the producer base is shrinking at historic speed. Active U.S. craft distillers fell from 3,069 to 2,282 in 12 months through August 2025—a 25.6% decline. In Scotland, Diageo mothballed Teaninich Distillery and suspended production at Roseisle Maltings; Speyside Distillery permanently ceased operations; Isle of Harris and Lindores Abbey cut staff. Major U.S. producers are in retrenchment: Jim Beam suspended all distilling at Clermont, Kentucky for the entirety of 2026; Brown-Forman cut 12% of its global workforce and closed its Louisville cooperage; Diageo’s North American sales fell 2.5% and the CEO departed. American whiskey exports, once reaching record $2.4 billion in 2024, collapsed in Q2 2025—down 85% to Canada, 25% to the UK, 23% to Japan, and 12% to the EU following retaliatory tariff escalation.

But contraction for some is opportunity for others. Irish whiskey exports crossed €1 billion in 2024, up 13%. Don Julio overtook Tito’s as the #1 U.S. spirits brand by retail value at $2.6 billion. RTD spirits-based cocktails grew 16.4% to $3.8 billion. And the distressed-asset pipeline—from mothballed Scottish distilleries to Stoli Group’s Chapter 7 liquidation ($100–500M in assets) to MGP Ingredients’ 68% gross profit collapse—presents acquisition opportunities at 30–60% discounts to peak valuations. This analysis maps the crisis and identifies where forward-looking capital should deploy.

The Inventory Overhang: The Underreported Risk

16.1 million barrels of American whiskey sit aging in Kentucky warehouses as of January 2025—triple the 2012 level. Barrel taxes alone cost the industry $75 million in 2025, up 27% in a single year. Through August 2025, industry-wide whiskey production fell 28% versus the prior year. In Mexico, the Tequila Regulatory Council reported 525 million liters of tequila in barrel or awaiting bottling at end-2023—nearly one full year of production. Diageo’s inventory-to-revenue ratio rose from 34% (FY2022) to 43% (2025), with the value of aged stock reaching $8.6 billion. When Diageo, Beam Suntory, and Brown-Forman resume production at idle facilities, that will be the clearest signal the supply-demand balance has normalized. Currently, those facilities are dark through at least mid-2026.

Key Trade Relationships

Each producing region faces distinct tariff and sentiment dynamics.

! U.S. → EU

Bourbon currently enters duty-free after April 2025 exclusion—but the arrangement is not a treaty and can be reversed. DISCUS estimates $600M+ in cumulative lost export revenue since 2018. European consumers shifted to Irish and local whiskey during the tariff years. Shelf space recovery will be slow even with zero tariff.

! U.S. → Canada

Exports collapsed 85% in Q2 2025 to $9.6M. Brown-Forman reported 62% Canada sales decline. Ontario LCBO pulled all U.S. spirits; over $80M in product sits in Ontario warehouses. CEO Lawson Whiting: physical product removal “worse than a tariff.” Among Canadians over 60, 84% say they are “very unlikely” to purchase U.S. alcohol in the future.

⚠ U.S. ↔ Mexico

USMCA-compliant tequila and mezcal are exempt from the 25% Mexico tariff. This is the category’s moat. U.S. tequila imports: $5.2B in 2024. Agave spirits represent 22% of U.S. beverage alcohol revenue. WSWA estimates tariff exposure would endanger 14,000 American jobs and $2.5B in economic output. USMCA review in 2026 introduces renegotiation risk.

! U.S. ↔ France

20% U.S. tariff on EU goods (April 2025), adjusted to 15% (August 2025). FEVS projected 20% decline in French spirits U.S. sales. Simultaneously, China imposed 27–35% anti-dumping duties on EU brandy (July 2025). From January to May 2025, Chinese imports of EU brandy fell 60% in volume, 75% in value. Cognac squeezed from two directions.

⚠ Russia & Eastern Europe

Post-Ukraine invasion: UK Russian Standard sales collapsed 75% in two weeks. NielsenIQ estimated £111M market gap. Stoli rebranded, relocated to Luxembourg, went 100% non-Russian sourcing—then filed Chapter 7 bankruptcy in the U.S. Beluga relocated production to Montenegro. Russian Standard pursuing India, Nigeria, China distribution via Allied Blenders and NEXT International.

⚠ Anti-American Sentiment

62% of global marketers at U.S. multinationals report anti-American sentiment is hurting overseas sales (AMA New York/Gravity Research, 2025). Anti-Americanism on French social media surged 5–10x in early 2025. Canada’s boycott is the most operationally severe consumer sentiment event in modern spirits export history.

III. Category-by-Category: Crisis & Opportunity

Where each category sits in the restructuring. Named brands, companies, and data points.

American Whiskey (Bourbon & Rye)

Revenue declined 1.8% to $5.2B. Two consecutive years of contraction—first time in over two decades.

The inventory overhang is the story. 16.1 million barrels aging in Kentucky—triple the 2012 level. Barrel taxes up 27% to ~$75M. Brown-Forman (Jack Daniel’s) cut 12% of its global workforce and closed its cooperage, projecting $70–80M in savings. Jim Beam suspended all distilling at Clermont for 2026. MGP Ingredients, the industry bellwether for bulk whiskey, saw gross profits collapse 68%. Through August 2025, industry-wide whiskey production fell 28%.

Exports: American whiskey accounted for 54% of all U.S. spirits exports at $1.3B in 2024 but fell 13% in Q2 2025. The EU currently offers duty-free access, but the political fragility of that arrangement means the export thesis remains contingent. Canada is effectively closed. Brown-Forman reported a 62% sales decline in Canada; CFO Leanne Cunningham called it “a significant impact to our top-line performance.”

Headwinds

  • 16.1M barrel overhang: decade of overinvestment meeting declining demand
  • Jim Beam, Heaven Hill, Maker’s Mark, Wild Turkey all reduced production
  • Canada exports −85%; Brown-Forman Canada −62%
  • Entry-price bourbon losing to RTD and tequila
  • Ultra-premium segment decelerated to +6% (down from double digits)

Where Value Remains

  • Distressed craft acquisition targets at 30–50% of peak valuations
  • Bulk whiskey at historic lows (MGP restructuring)
  • Woodford Reserve and Maker’s Mark premium positioning holding
  • Contract distilling capacity at Lawrenceburg available at discount
  • EU duty-free access, if it holds, still the #1 export opportunity

Scotch Whisky

Global exports £5.4B (down 3.7% in value) but volume up 3.9%. Price mix compressing across the supply chain.

India is now the #1 Scotch export market by volume (192 million bottles, +13.8% value). China collapsed: −31.5%, dropping from 5th to 10th most valuable market. The U.S.—still #1 by value—faces a new 10% tariff that Diageo quantified at ~$200M annual exposure. Pernod Ricard estimated combined U.S./China tariff impact at ~$207M. A 750mL bottle faces ~$1.92 in port-level tariff translating to over $12 at bar retail.

The UK-India FTA is the single largest latent catalyst. India’s 150% whisky import tariff, if reduced, could unlock £1 billion in incremental Scotch export value over five years per the SWA. Meanwhile, Port Ellen (Islay) reopened after 40 years and Rosebank completed its first distillation—confirming that ultra-premium investment continues even as mass-market contracts.

Irish Whiskey

Exports crossed €1B in 2024 (+13%). The structural winner in global whiskey—with caveats.

Irish whiskey exports recovered to €1.03 billion in 2024, gaining share in the UK (+8%), India (+23%), and Japan (+27%). Jameson sold 10.7 million cases globally in FY2024 and holds ~77% U.S. share and ~70% globally. India grew 52% and China 99% for the brand. Bushmills committed £70M to expand maturation capacity from 1.6M to 4.5M cases/year. The category has grown from 4 operational distilleries in 2010 to approximately 50 by 2024.

The caveat: over 90% of Irish distilleries have paused or reduced production by mid-2025. Midleton itself paused production in March 2025. Waterford Whisky shut down November 2024. Killarney and Nephin entered liquidation. The boom overbuilt supply infrastructure that the current demand environment cannot support. Jameson’s concentration risk is real: when one brand represents 70%+ of a category, “category growth” is really single-brand performance.

Why Irish Wins Now

  • Zero tariff exposure to EU/UK—direct advantage over bourbon
  • Exports €1B+ in 2024, up 13% after recovering from 14% prior-year dip
  • Jameson: India +52%, China +99%—genuine emerging market diversification
  • Bushmills £70M expansion: CVC Capital Partners signaling medium-term confidence
  • Accessible pricing ($25–45) in the sweet spot identified in brand success analysis

Risks to Monitor

  • 90%+ of distilleries paused or reduced production mid-2025
  • Waterford, Killarney, Nephin closures show long tail is vulnerable
  • Jameson = 70%+ of category; concentration risk is structural
  • U.S. control-state data shows Irish whiskey −8.3% in Jan 2026

Japanese Whisky

Supply scarcity as a moat. Suntory increased output 32% from 2018–2023, but exports grew 140% over the same period.

The fundamental math: demand consistently exceeds supply. Suntory invested ¥10 billion (~$63.5M) into Yamazaki and Hakushu. Storage capacity grew 50%+ since 2016. And it’s still not enough. 2021 labeling standards tightened what qualifies as “Japanese whisky,” further reducing apparent supply. The return of Nikka Yoichi 10—a sought-after age-statement expression—is a positive signal. Widespread availability of affordable, age-stated Japanese whisky is unlikely before 2030.

Export value did decline 12.8% in 2024, dragged by China, Hong Kong, and soft U.S. demand. U.S. exports fell 23% in Q2 2025 under the 10% Japan tariff. But premium Japanese expressions face lower demand elasticity than standard-tier products. The category remains investable at ultra-premium and collectible levels.

Tequila & Mezcal

Don Julio overtook Tito’s as #1 U.S. brand by retail value ($2.6B). But category deceleration, agave crash, and 75+ celebrity brands signal peak saturation.

Tequila/mezcal revenue grew 2.9% to $6.7B in 2024—a dramatic deceleration from the double-digit growth of 2020–2022. Don Julio (+28.2% to 4.4M cases) is the standout. Patrón fell to ~2.8M cases, lowest since pre-2020. Jose Cuervo declined 2% at ~4.92M cases. Casamigos faces authenticity lawsuits alongside Don Julio and 818 over “100% agave” labeling claims.

The agave story: spot prices crashed to MXN 2/kg (~$0.10) from peaks above MXN 25/kg. Registered growers more than quadrupled since 2018. A 500-million-liter agave surplus—“the Tequila Lake”—benefits buyers short-term but is devastating independent farming communities. Euromonitor projects −0.8% volume in 2025 but +6.2% value, confirming premiumization is the only surviving growth mechanism. USMCA protection is the category’s tariff moat, but the 2026 review introduces renegotiation risk.

Vodka

$7.2B revenue, still #1 by volume (~74M cases). Tito’s declined for the first time ever (−1.5% to ~12M cases).

The post-Russian brand displacement is permanent. Russian vodka imports fell 79% since 2011 to just 1.3% of U.S. imports before the Ukraine invasion accelerated the exit. Stoli’s Chapter 7 liquidation closes the chapter on the brand’s U.S. operations. The shelf space vacated by Russian brands was absorbed by existing American and European brands. Beluga relocated production to Montenegro. Russian Standard is pivoting to India and Nigeria.

The structural challenge: RTD spirits-based cocktails and tequila are capturing the occasions vodka once owned. Super-premium vodka declined 15% in 2024. The value segment has no floor. Tito’s—at $2.5B retail—holds extraordinary market share but even it contracted for the first time.

Cognac & Brandy

Category in crisis. Volumes collapsed 41% from 8.5M cases (2021) to ~5M cases. Squeezed simultaneously by U.S. and Chinese tariffs.

Hennessy holds 62.3% U.S. category share at 3.2M cases (+2%—the only top-five brand to grow). Rémy Martin −21.2% to 622K cases. D’Ussé −18% to 525K cases. Courvoisier −4.5% to 406K cases. LVMH reported 17% lower cognac/spirits revenue in Q1 2025.

China anti-dumping duties (27–35%, effective July 2025) are devastating. Chinese imports of EU brandy fell 60% in volume and 75% in value from January to May 2025. Major houses (Hennessy, Rémy, Martell) received duty exemptions conditioned on minimum price floors set by Beijing—an unusual government-administered pricing mechanism. BNIC data: XO shipments fell 26.4%, while VS rose 13.7%, representing a massive downgrade in revenue quality. Investment thesis is binary: depends entirely on simultaneous U.S. and China trade resolution.

Rum: The Sleeper Opportunity

Standard rum declining, but ultra-premium rum growing 12% annually since 2020. Global luxury rum market: $1.64B, projected $2.82B by 2032 (8.2% CAGR).

Caribbean rum producers have minimal tariff exposure in most Western markets, lower production costs than Scottish or American distillers, and authentic provenance narratives (Barbados, Jamaica, Guatemala). Foursquare (Barbados) has emerged as the cult distillery equivalent of whisky’s allocated releases—secondary market prices rival premium Scotch. Diplomatico, Appleton Estate (Campari), Mount Gay (Bacardi), and Flor de Caña are gaining share in the $30–80 premium segment. Tropical aging accelerates maturation, meaning faster capital cycles than Scotch. Rhum Agricole (Martinique AOC) provides defensible quality designations comparable to whisky appellations.

Gin: Two Markets, Two Stories

U.S. declining (−3%). UK declining (−3.2% volume). Flavored gin segment collapsed. Premium IWSR forecast: −5% CAGR through 2028.

The UK flavored gin boom (2018–2021) is over. Gordon’s (Diageo) grew 9.5% in 2024 by pivoting to 0.0% ABV variants. Hendrick’s (William Grant) and Bombay Sapphire (Bacardi) maintain premium positions but the addressable market is shrinking. UK gin is a net beneficiary of the American spirits trade disruption in its home market—as UK consumers reduce bourbon purchases due to tariff-driven price increases, that spending partly redirects to domestic gin. But this is a rearrangement within a declining category, not growth.

$ India: The Growth Market

Indian whisky: 260M cases (2024), projected 502M by 2034 (7.6% CAGR). McDowell’s No.1 sold 32.2M cases; Royal Stag 31M cases (+11.1%). India overtook France as #1 Scotch market by volume. UK-India FTA could cut 150% import tariff—the single largest latent catalyst in global spirits. Indian conglomerates (Tilaknagar, Radico Khaitan) expanding international footprint.

VI. Investment Thesis: Where to Deploy Capital

Four actionable themes for the 3–5 year horizon.

HIGH CONVICTION

1. Irish Whiskey Infrastructure

Best risk/reward in spirits. Tariff-free EU/UK access, consumer preference shift, Jameson’s India +52% / China +99% growth vectors. Entry points: emerging distilleries with aged stock and export capability, or mid-tier brands being shed by Pernod’s restructuring (Knappogue Castle model). Bushmills £70M expansion signals category-level confidence from sophisticated capital.

HIGH CONVICTION

2. Premium Caribbean Rum

Undervalued. Minimal tariff exposure, authentic provenance, 12% annual ultra-premium growth since 2020, whiskey-crossover consumer bridge in place. Tropical aging = faster capital cycles than Scotch. Target: Barbadian, Jamaican, Guatemalan distilleries with aged stock. The Foursquare effect is creating cult demand. Projected $2.82B luxury rum market by 2032.

HIGH CONVICTION

3. RTD Spirits-Based

Only high-growth segment at scale: +16.4% to $3.8B. Cannibalizing entry-price spirits and beer. Capital efficient (brand + contract production). RTD spirits share still <10% of total—significant runway. Key risk is maturation, but High Noon and Surfside prove the model scales. Look for differentiated brands with distribution partnerships.

MEDIUM CONVICTION

4. Distressed U.S. & Scottish Assets

Attractive pricing (30–60% discount to peak) but requires operational expertise and patience. U.S. craft roll-ups need distribution infrastructure. Scottish mothballed distilleries offer maturing stock + tourism but demand is uncertain. Stoli/Kentucky Owl liquidation is highest-profile opportunity. Best for strategic acquirers or experienced spirits PE operators, not generalist investors.

Capital Traps to Avoid

  • Betting on bourbon export recovery without tariff certainty: EU access is by political contingency, not treaty. The 2026 environment can reverse at any point. Don’t invest in export-dependent American whiskey capacity until trade frameworks are durable.
  • Celebrity spirits brand acquisitions: 75+ celebrity tequilas. Most have zero enterprise value independent of their famous founder. Casamigos, Don Julio, and 818 face class-action lawsuits over 100% agave claims. Unless the brand has genuine scan data velocity, pass.
  • Cognac without trade conviction: Recovery depends on simultaneous U.S. and China resolution. Rémy Cointreau’s XO exposure (−26.4% volume) is acute. Only for investors with specific trade-policy insight.
  • Undifferentiated craft spirits: 89% of craft producers share 11% of revenue. Acquiring a brand without secured distribution is acquiring a brand without a business.
  • Value vodka: Super-premium vodka down 15% in 2024. The segment is in permanent structural decline from RTD substitution, health trends, and tequila migration.
Conclusion

The Map Has Changed. Read the New One.

The global spirits industry is not collapsing—it is restructuring. Capital that was concentrated in American whiskey exports, Scottish distillery expansion, and celebrity brand launches is being released and redirected. The $340 billion global market is not shrinking; it is reallocating. The investors who profit will be those who stop waiting for the old map to come back.
