Global Spirits: Crisis, Capital & Opportunity

An investor’s guide to where capital and opportunity are moving as the global spirits industry restructures over the next 3–5 years.

Investment Thesis

The Great Spirits Restructuring

The global spirits industry is experiencing its most significant structural realignment since Prohibition. Six simultaneous forces are creating a once-in-a-generation opportunity landscape for investors who can read the map:

▼ U.S. De-Premiumization + ✖ Retaliatory Tariff Walls + 🏭 Accelerating Closures + 🌍 Geopolitical Fractures + 💰 Distressed Asset Prices = → Capital Reallocation Event

Executive Summary

The global spirits industry is in its most significant restructuring since Prohibition. U.S. supplier revenues are down to $37.2 billion from a ~$39.1 billion peak, American whiskey exports collapsed across every major market in 2025, and the producer base is contracting at historic speed on both sides of the Atlantic. The details of the contraction are well documented. This analysis is about what comes next.

Where the opportunities are:

  • Acquire or invest in Irish whiskey infrastructure — Irish whiskey is capturing the export market share that tariff-burdened American whiskey is losing. The category has tariff-free EU/UK access, a proven emerging-market growth engine (Jameson +52% India, +99% China), and a wave of mid-tier distilleries that overbuilt during the boom and now need capital partners. This is the best risk-adjusted entry point in global spirits.
  • Build or acquire RTD spirits brands with distribution partnerships — RTD is the only segment growing at scale (+16.4% to $3.8B) and it’s cannibalizing both entry-price spirits and beer. The category is still under 10% of total spirits—the runway is long. Capital-efficient model (brand + contract production) means you don’t need to own a distillery to compete.
  • Position in premium Caribbean rum before the whiskey-crossover trade matures — Ultra-premium rum is growing 12% annually with minimal tariff exposure, lower production costs, and faster capital cycles than Scotch (tropical aging). The whiskey-to-rum crossover consumer is real but the category is still under-invested. First movers in Barbadian, Jamaican, and Guatemalan aged rum assets will set the terms.
  • Buy distressed American and Scottish production assets at cycle-bottom pricing — Mothballed Scottish distilleries are available at 40–60% of replacement cost with maturing stock included. U.S. craft operations at 30–50% of peak. Stoli’s Chapter 7 puts $100–500M in assets on the market. MGP’s collapse signals a buyer’s market for bulk whiskey. These assets don’t require a recovery thesis—they require operational expertise and patient capital.
  • Establish distribution infrastructure in India — India’s whisky market (260M cases, projected 502M by 2034) is where the next generation of global spirits volume lives. A UK-India FTA cutting the 150% import tariff would be a $1.3B catalyst for Scotch alone. Capital deployed into Indian distribution relationships now will compound as the market opens.
  • Acquire premium tequila brands with real retail velocity — The celebrity brand glut (75+ tequilas) is creating forced sellers, but Don Julio’s rise to #1 U.S. brand by retail value ($2.6B) proves premiumization still works where the story is authentic. Target heritage and additive-free brands with actual NielsenIQ scan data, not celebrity names with PR claims. The agave price crash ($0.10/kg from $1.25) means input costs favor buyers.

This analysis maps where forward-looking capital should deploy.

I. The Landscape: Where Things Stand

Volume up, revenue down. The market is trading down, not drying up—and the gaps it’s creating are the opportunity.

Distillery Closures & Major Producer Retrenchment

Named closures, bankruptcies, and production halts. This is the distressed asset pipeline.

U.S. Closures & Bankruptcies (2024–2025)

  • Stoli Group USA & Kentucky Owl — Chapter 11 (Nov 2024), converted to Chapter 7 liquidation (Jan 2026). $100–500M assets; $78M secured debt to Fifth Third Bank
  • Devils River Distillery (San Antonio) — Chapter 11, May 2025. Distribution in 36 states
  • Boston Harbor Distillery — Chapter 11, March 2025. $500K–1M assets
  • Montana Distillery — Chapter 11, closed March 2025. Regulatory constraints (2 oz/day tasting limit)
  • JJ Pfister Distilling (Sacramento) — Closed Nov 2024, Chapter 11 filed May 2025. $1.6M debt
  • Black Button Distilling (Rochester, NY) — Acquired by Blackstar Sept 2025; staff fired, liquidated by Nov 2025
  • Brown-Forman: 648 layoffs (12% workforce), Louisville cooperage closed. $70–80M annual savings target
  • Jim Beam (Suntory): Full production suspension at Clermont, KY for all of 2026
  • Diageo: Halted Lebanon, KY distillery; $625M cost-cutting program; CEO Debra Crew departed
  • MGP Ingredients: Gross profits fell 68% in 2024; cutting whiskey output 2026

Scotland & Ireland (2024–2025)

  • Teaninich Distillery (Diageo, Highland) — Production paused; feeds Johnnie Walker blends
  • Roseisle Maltings (Diageo, Speyside) — Suspended through June 2026+; $51M facility opened 2009
  • Speyside Distillery (Kingussie) — Permanently ceased distilling May 2025; “ghost distillery” status
  • Isle of Harris Distillery — Production pause, job cuts April 2025, citing tariff uncertainty
  • Lindores Abbey Distillery (Fife) — Staff redundancies amid closure rumors
  • Glenglassaugh & BenRiach (Brown-Forman) — Shared production model reducing capacity
  • Waterford Whisky (Ireland) — Shut down November 2024
  • Midleton Distillery (Irish Distillers/Pernod) — Production paused March 2025; $270M expansion delayed
  • Killarney Brewing & Distilling (Ireland) — Closed 2024–2025
  • Nephin Whiskey (Ireland) — Liquidator appointed

The Recovery Signal to Watch

16.1 million barrels aging in Kentucky, 525 million liters of tequila in Mexican inventory, Diageo’s inventory-to-revenue ratio at 43%. The overhang is well documented. What matters for investors is the signal: when Jim Beam, Teaninich, Roseisle, and Midleton restart production, the supply-demand balance has normalized. Until then, the buyer’s market for bulk whiskey and distressed assets remains open.

II. Trade & Tariff Environment

The Supreme Court struck down IEEPA tariffs in February 2026—but the administration replaced them within 24 hours, and retaliatory measures from trading partners remain in force.

Retaliatory Tariff Timeline

American spirits as collateral damage in broader trade disputes.

Escalation / damage Uncertain / mixed Relief / opportunity Structural shift
June 2018
EU imposes 25% retaliatory tariff on American whiskey
Response to U.S. steel/aluminum tariffs. Bourbon exports to EU decline 20% over three years ($552M to $439M).
January 2022
EU suspends 25% tariff under bilateral metals agreement
American whiskey exports to EU recover from $439M to $699M by 2024 (+60%).
February–March 2025
Multi-front retaliation begins
Canada: 25% retaliatory tariff + Ontario LCBO pulls all U.S. spirits from shelves. EU proposes 50% tariff on bourbon in $103B retaliation package. Record $2.4B in 2024 exports revealed as front-loading, not organic demand.
April 2025
EU excludes bourbon from final retaliatory list
DISCUS lobbying and “Toasts Not Tariffs” coalition succeed. Bourbon retains duty-free EU access. EU suspends retaliatory tariffs on U.S. spirits through February 2026.
Q2 2025
Export cliff materializes despite EU exemption
Total exports −9% YoY. Canada −85%. UK −25%. Japan −23%. EU −12%. Damage driven by Canada shelf bans, UK/Japan tariffs, and post-stockpile hangover in EU.
August 2025
Canada lifts federal tariff; provincial shelf bans persist
Canada removed its 25% retaliatory tariff—but Ontario and other provinces kept American spirits off LCBO shelves. Brown-Forman CEO: physical removal is “worse than a tariff.” Over $80M in U.S. product sits in Ontario warehouses.
February 20, 2026
Supreme Court strikes down IEEPA tariffs (Learning Resources v. Trump)
6–3 ruling. Court holds IEEPA does not authorize broad tariffs: “IEEPA contains no reference to tariffs or duties.” V.O.S. Selections, a wine and spirits importer, was a companion case. DISCUS CEO Chris Swonger urged the administration to “secure a permanent return to zero-for-zero tariffs.”
February 24, 2026
Trump replaces IEEPA tariffs with Section 122 surcharge within 24 hours
10% global tariff surcharge under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974, with stated intent to raise to 15%. Valid for 150 days (expires ~July 2026), requires congressional approval to extend. Section 232 (steel/aluminum) and Section 301 (China) tariffs unaffected by ruling.
March 2026 — Current
Legal limbo: IEEPA gone, Section 122 in effect, retaliatory measures persist
EU retaliatory tariff suspension expired Feb 5, 2026; reimposition delayed pending negotiations but 50% threat not withdrawn. Canada provincial bans entering second year. UK Boeing-Airbus Scotch tariff suspension expires June 2026. SCOTUS ruling is political leverage, not commercial resolution.

III. Category-by-Category: Crisis & Opportunity

Where each category sits in the restructuring. Named brands, companies, and data points.

American Whiskey (Bourbon & Rye)

Revenue declined 1.8% to $5.2B. Two consecutive years of contraction—first time in over two decades. The closures and export collapse are covered above. Here’s what it means for capital.

Headwinds

  • 16.1M barrel overhang: decade of overinvestment meeting declining demand
  • Jim Beam, Heaven Hill, Maker’s Mark, Wild Turkey all reduced production
  • Canada effectively closed: U.S. spirits exports down 85% (Q2 2025); provincial shelf bans entering second year
  • Entry-price bourbon losing to RTD and tequila
  • Ultra-premium segment decelerated to +6% (down from double digits)

Where Value Remains

  • Distressed craft acquisition targets at 30–50% of peak valuations
  • Bulk whiskey at historic lows (MGP restructuring)
  • Woodford Reserve and Maker’s Mark premium positioning holding
  • Contract distilling capacity at Lawrenceburg available at discount
  • EU duty-free access, if it holds, still the #1 export opportunity

Scotch Whisky

Global exports $6.9B (down 3.7% value, up 3.9% volume). Selling more bottles at lower prices.

Headwinds

  • China collapsed −31.5%, dropping from 5th to 10th most valuable export market
  • 10% U.S. tariff on UK imports raising Scotch prices at retail—a 750mL bottle sees ~$12 markup by the time it reaches the bar
  • Blended Scotch commoditized; losing share to Irish whiskey globally
  • Teaninich, Roseisle, Speyside closures signal oversupply
  • Boeing-Airbus 25% tariff on single malt was suspended in 2021 for five years—that suspension expires June 2026 with no renewal in place

Where Value Remains

  • India now #1 export market by volume (192M bottles, +13.8% value)
  • UK-India FTA could unlock $1.3B in incremental Scotch exports over five years (SWA)
  • Mothballed distilleries available at 40–60% of replacement cost with maturing stock
  • Port Ellen and Rosebank reopenings confirm ultra-premium investment continues
  • Single malt collectible segment appreciating as production pauses reduce future supply

Irish Whiskey

Exports crossed $1.1B in 2024 (+13%). The structural winner in global whiskey—with caveats.

Why Irish Wins Now

  • Zero tariff exposure to EU/UK—direct advantage over bourbon
  • Exports $1.1B+ in 2024, up 13% after recovering from 14% prior-year dip
  • Jameson: India +52%, China +99%—genuine emerging market diversification
  • Bushmills $89M expansion: CVC Capital Partners signaling medium-term confidence
  • Accessible pricing ($25–45) in the sweet spot identified in brand success analysis

Risks to Monitor

  • 90%+ of distilleries paused or reduced production mid-2025
  • Waterford, Killarney, Nephin closures show long tail is vulnerable
  • Jameson = 70%+ of category; concentration risk is structural
  • U.S. control-state data shows Irish whiskey −8.3% in Jan 2026

Japanese Whisky

Supply scarcity as a moat. Suntory increased output 32% (2018–2023) but exports grew 140%. Demand still exceeds supply.

Headwinds

  • Export value declined 12.8% in 2024; China, Hong Kong, soft U.S. demand
  • U.S. exports fell 23% in Q2 2025 under 10% Japan tariff
  • Limited acquisition targets: Suntory and Nikka dominate; few independents
  • Production lead times measured in decades, not years
  • “Japanese-style” whiskies from other countries creating category confusion

Where Value Remains

  • Supply scarcity maintains pricing power; affordable age-stated bottles unlikely before 2030
  • Suntory invested $64M in Yamazaki and Hakushu; storage capacity up 50%+
  • 2021 labeling standards tightened definitions, further reducing compliant supply
  • Nikka Yoichi 10 return signals easing constraints at select price points
  • Secondary market appreciation creates collector/investor demand layer

Tequila & Mezcal

Revenue +2.9% to $6.7B (2024). Don Julio now #1 U.S. brand by retail value ($2.6B). Growth decelerating sharply from double-digit era.

Headwinds

  • 75+ celebrity tequilas competing for finite shelf space; consumer fatigue setting in
  • Patrón fell to ~2.8M cases (lowest since pre-2020); Cuervo −2%
  • Casamigos, Don Julio, 818 facing class-action lawsuits over “100% agave” claims
  • Agave price crash ($0.10/kg from $1.25) devastating farming communities; future supply risk
  • USMCA review in 2026 introduces renegotiation risk to tariff moat
  • 500M-liter surplus (“the Tequila Lake”) signals category-level overproduction

Where Value Remains

  • Don Julio +28.2% to 4.4M cases—premiumization still works with authentic brands
  • USMCA tariff moat intact: tequila/mezcal exempt from 25% Mexico tariff
  • Agave crash benefits buyers with long-term supply contracts (input costs at historic lows)
  • Additive-free certified brands growing at 10x category rate
  • Mezcal still growing off small base; artisanal positioning insulates from celebrity glut
  • Euromonitor: +6.2% value in 2025 even as volume dips −0.8%

Vodka

$7.2B revenue, still #1 by volume (~74M cases). Tito’s declined for the first time ever (−1.5% to ~12M cases).

Headwinds

  • RTD and tequila capturing occasions vodka once owned
  • Super-premium vodka declined 15% in 2024; value segment has no floor
  • Tito’s ($2.5B retail) contracted for the first time—even the category leader can’t grow
  • Smirnoff −6%; category commoditized at every tier below Tito’s
  • Health and moderation trends disproportionately impact high-pour categories

Where Value Remains

  • Post-Russian shelf space reallocation is permanent; Stoli Chapter 7 closes the door
  • Polish vodka (Belvedere, Chopin) gaining premium positioning in vacated space
  • Vodka-based RTD still growing despite base category decline
  • Beluga relocated to Montenegro; Russian Standard pivoting to India, Nigeria—non-Western gaps exist
  • Minimal export tariff exposure (Tito’s is domestic; limited international footprint)

Cognac & Brandy

Volumes collapsed 41% from 8.5M cases (2021) to ~5M cases. Squeezed by U.S. and Chinese tariffs simultaneously.

Headwinds

  • Rémy Martin −21.2%; D’Ussé −18%; Courvoisier −4.5%; LVMH cognac −17% Q1 2025
  • China anti-dumping duties (27–35%): EU brandy imports fell 60% volume, 75% value (Jan–May 2025)
  • XO shipments −26.4% while VS +13.7%—massive revenue quality downgrade
  • Major houses granted duty exemptions conditioned on Beijing-set minimum price floors
  • U.S. 15% tariff on EU goods further compressing margins

Where Value Remains

  • Hennessy: 62.3% U.S. share, 3.2M cases, +2%—the only top-five brand growing
  • Courvoisier acquired by Campari ($1.2B)—strategic buyer sees long-term value
  • Rémy Cointreau stock −40% from peak—attractive if you have trade-policy conviction
  • Category is a binary bet: simultaneous U.S. + China tariff resolution = significant upside
  • BNIC reduced harvest yields—supply discipline could support pricing recovery

Rum

Standard rum declining, but ultra-premium growing 12% annually since 2020. Luxury rum market: $1.64B, projected $2.82B by 2032 (8.2% CAGR).

Headwinds

  • Overall U.S. rum volume declining; standard segment (Captain Morgan, Bacardi Superior) losing to RTD
  • Category perception still skews cheap/mixer in most markets
  • Fragmented production across dozens of Caribbean nations complicates scale
  • Limited marketing infrastructure compared to whiskey and tequila
  • Sugar price volatility affects input costs

Where Value Remains

  • Minimal tariff exposure in most Western markets; sits outside geopolitical fault lines
  • Foursquare (Barbados) emerging as cult equivalent of allocated bourbon—secondary prices rival Scotch
  • Diplomatico, Appleton Estate, Mount Gay, Flor de Caña gaining share in $30–80 premium segment
  • Tropical aging accelerates maturation = faster capital cycles than Scotch
  • Whiskey-to-rum crossover consumer is real and growing
  • Rhum Agricole (Martinique AOC) provides defensible quality designations

Gin

U.S. −3%. UK −3.2% volume. Flavored gin segment collapsed. Premium IWSR forecast: −5% CAGR through 2028.

Headwinds

  • UK flavored gin boom (2018–2021) is definitively over; segment fragmented beyond recovery
  • U.S. market mature with no innovation catalyst visible
  • Premium tier forecast −5% CAGR through 2028 (IWSR)
  • Addressable market shrinking in both major markets simultaneously

Where Value Remains

  • Gordon’s +9.5% (2024) by pivoting to 0.0% ABV—no/low is the gin growth story
  • Hendrick’s and Bombay Sapphire holding premium positions
  • UK gin benefits from bourbon shelf-space exit as tariff-driven prices redirect spending
  • Limited opportunity overall—a rearrangement within a declining category, not growth

IV. Forward-Looking Indicators & Capital Flow

Where investment money is actually moving. The signals that separate recovery from permanent restructuring.

Where Capital Is Actually Moving

55 beverage alcohol acquisitions in 2024, 51 in 2025. Spirits led both years. The pattern: majors divesting, mid-tier acquiring.

$ Irish Distillery Investment

Bushmills: $89M expansion to 4.5M cases capacity (CVC Capital Partners). Pernod Ricard’s $270M Midleton expansion delayed but committed. European PE firms and Asian beverage companies investing in Irish assets. Category attracting capital that previously flowed to Scottish and American projects.

$ Major Divestitures Creating Opportunity

Diageo: Sold Cacique rum to La Martiniquaise; transferred Cîroc to Main Street Advisors; sold East African Breweries to Asahi ($2.3B); halted Distill Ventures incubator. Campari: Bought Courvoisier ($1.2B) while shedding Cinzano ($108M), Averna and Zedda Piras ($108M). Pernod: Sold Campo Viejo, Jacob’s Creek, Knappogue Castle. Targeting $1.1B in cuts by FY2029.

$ RTD / Spirits-Based

+16.4% revenue to $3.8B in 2025. The only high-growth segment at scale. High Noon (Gallo), Surfside (Stateside), Cutwater (AB InBev). Cannibalizing entry-price spirits and beer simultaneously. RTD share still <10% of total spirits—runway remains. Capital efficient: brand + contract production.

$ No/Low ABV Emerging

Growing 10% annually. Non-alcoholic spirits projected $1.2B by 2034 (7.4% CAGR). Diageo acquired Ritual Zero Proof. Rémy Cointreau took stake in JNPR Spirits. AG Barr acquired Strykk. 61 million new global consumers entered no-alcohol segment in 2024 (IWSR). Strategic portfolio insurance more than core growth play.

$ Mid-Tier Roll-Up Wave

Uncle Nearest acquired Square One Organic Spirits. Heritage Distilling acquired Thinking Tree Spirits. Redwood Empire Whiskey grew 30% and acquired Savage & Cooke distillery. Next Century Spirits acquiring orphaned brands from Diageo, Beam, and Pernod divestitures. Early transactions of what may become significant mid-market consolidation.

$ India: The Growth Market

Indian whisky: 260M cases (2024), projected 502M by 2034 (7.6% CAGR). McDowell’s No.1 sold 32.2M cases; Royal Stag 31M cases (+11.1%). India overtook France as #1 Scotch market by volume. UK-India FTA could cut 150% import tariff—the single largest latent catalyst in global spirits. Indian conglomerates (Tilaknagar, Radico Khaitan) expanding international footprint.

Recovery vs. Permanent Structural Shift

Seven indicators to monitor. Currently: five red, two amber, zero green. This pattern favors structural shift over cyclical recovery.

1
EU-U.S. Tariff Resolution
SCOTUS struck down IEEPA tariffs (Feb 2026) but Section 122 replaced them. EU retaliatory suspension expired; 50% threat not withdrawn. Watch for permanent zero-for-zero deal.
2
Canada Market Access
Federal tariff lifted Sept 2025 but provincial bans entering second year (March 2026). 3,600+ products pulled from LCBO. SCOTUS ruling has not changed provincial response. Sentiment recovery will lag further.
3
U.S. Domestic Volume Inflection
WSWA SipSource: 2025 tracking toward 3rd consecutive negative year for core spirits depletions. Watch for two consecutive quarters of positive growth.
4
Production Restart at Idle Facilities
Jim Beam, Teaninich, Roseisle, Midleton, George Dickel, Balcones all dark through mid-2026+. When they restart, supply-demand has normalized.
5
China Luxury Spirits Recovery
Scotch to China −31.5%. Cognac to China −24% value. Anti-dumping duties formalized. No clear recovery timeline.
6
Craft Distillery Net Openings
Net −787 in 12 months (25.6% decline). Return to net positive openings would signal entrepreneurial confidence. Currently accelerating negative.
7
Gen Z Consumption Trajectory
Entering legal drinking age with materially lower consumption rates than Millennials. If this cohort ages into moderation, it’s a 15–25 year structural drag on total volume.
Reading the dashboard: As of March 2026, five of seven indicators are red (crisis/declining), two are amber (fragile/mixed). Zero are green. IWSR characterizes the status spirits decline as “more cyclical than structural”—but the Gen Z consumption data and inventory overhang suggest the recovery, when it comes, will restore a smaller market at lower margin than the 2019–2022 peak. For investors: deploy capital into categories and regions that are structurally gaining (Irish whiskey, premium rum, Japanese whisky, RTD, India) rather than betting on a full return to the pre-2018 American spirits export paradigm.

VI. Investment Thesis: Where to Deploy Capital

Four actionable themes for the 3–5 year horizon.

HIGH CONVICTION

1. Irish Whiskey Infrastructure

Best risk/reward in spirits. Tariff-free EU/UK access, consumer preference shift, Jameson’s India +52% / China +99% growth vectors. Entry points: emerging distilleries with aged stock and export capability, or mid-tier brands being shed by Pernod’s restructuring (Knappogue Castle model). Bushmills $89M expansion signals category-level confidence from sophisticated capital.

HIGH CONVICTION

2. Premium Caribbean Rum

Undervalued. Minimal tariff exposure, authentic provenance, 12% annual ultra-premium growth since 2020, whiskey-crossover consumer bridge in place. Tropical aging = faster capital cycles than Scotch. Target: Barbadian, Jamaican, Guatemalan distilleries with aged stock. The Foursquare effect is creating cult demand. Projected $2.82B luxury rum market by 2032.

HIGH CONVICTION

3. RTD Spirits-Based

Only high-growth segment at scale: +16.4% to $3.8B. Cannibalizing entry-price spirits and beer. Capital efficient (brand + contract production). RTD spirits share still <10% of total—significant runway. Key risk is maturation, but High Noon and Surfside prove the model scales. Look for differentiated brands with distribution partnerships.

MEDIUM CONVICTION

4. Distressed U.S. & Scottish Assets

Attractive pricing (30–60% discount to peak) but requires operational expertise and patience. U.S. craft roll-ups need distribution infrastructure. Scottish mothballed distilleries offer maturing stock + tourism but demand is uncertain. Stoli/Kentucky Owl liquidation is highest-profile opportunity. Best for strategic acquirers or experienced spirits PE operators, not generalist investors.

Capital Traps to Avoid

  • Betting on bourbon export recovery without a durable trade deal: The SCOTUS ruling struck down IEEPA tariffs but the administration replaced them with Section 122 within 24 hours. EU retaliatory suspension expired and the 50% threat remains. Don’t invest in export-dependent American whiskey capacity until a permanent zero-for-zero agreement is in place—not just a pause.
  • Celebrity spirits brand acquisitions: 75+ celebrity tequilas. Most have zero enterprise value independent of their famous founder. Casamigos, Don Julio, and 818 face class-action lawsuits over 100% agave claims. Unless the brand has genuine scan data velocity, pass.
  • Cognac without trade conviction: Recovery depends on simultaneous U.S. and China resolution. Rémy Cointreau’s XO exposure (−26.4% volume) is acute. Only for investors with specific trade-policy insight.
  • Undifferentiated craft spirits: 89% of craft producers share 11% of revenue. Acquiring a brand without secured distribution is acquiring a brand without a business.
  • Value vodka: Super-premium vodka down 15% in 2024. The segment is in permanent structural decline from RTD substitution, health trends, and tequila migration.
Conclusion

The Map Has Changed. Read the New One.

The global spirits industry is not collapsing—it is restructuring. Capital that was concentrated in American whiskey exports, Scottish distillery expansion, and celebrity brand launches is being released and redirected. The $340 billion global market is not shrinking; it is reallocating. The investors who profit will be those who stop waiting for the old map to come back.
